Classifying Materials into Liquids, Solids and Gases

What are the States of Matter?

All materials around us exist in three main states of matter: solid, liquid, and gas.

Solids

Fixed shape and volume

Liquids

Fixed volume, no fixed shape

Gases

No fixed shape or volume

Characteristics of Solids

Key Characteristics
Have a fixed shape and fixed volume
Cannot be easily compressed (squeezed)
Particles are packed closely together
Examples of Solids:
Stone, wood, chalk, books, desks, iron, glass, plastic bottle, shoe, phone
Natural solids: cocoa pod, yam, maize, salt, sugar

Characteristics of Liquids

Key Characteristics
Have a fixed volume but no fixed shape (take the shape of their container)
Can flow from one place to another
Cannot be easily compressed
Examples of Liquids:
Water, milk, cooking oil, blood, kerosene, soup, palm wine, juice
Natural liquids: coconut water, honey, rainwater

Characteristics of Gases

Key Characteristics
Have no fixed shape and no fixed volume
Can spread out to fill any space
Can be compressed (squeezed into smaller volume)
Examples of Gases:
Oxygen (O₂), carbon dioxide (CO₂), nitrogen, cooking gas (LPG), smoke, air in balloons, steam from boiling water

Importance of Liquids in Human Life

Why Liquids are Important

Liquids are very important because they:

Key Functions of Liquids:
Help in survival – water for drinking
Used in cooking – oil, soup, milk
Transport – petrol, diesel for cars, kerosene for lamps
Medicine – syrups and injections are liquid forms
Body functions – blood transports oxygen and nutrients
Cleaning – water is used for bathing, washing clothes, and cleaning homes
Teaching Tip

Discuss with students how their daily lives would be different without liquids. This helps them appreciate the importance of liquids in everyday life.

Examples of Liquids in Daily Life

At Home:
Water – for drinking, cooking, cleaning
Oil – for cooking, frying
Milk – for drinking, making tea, cooking
In School:
Ink – for writing
Sanitizer – for cleaning hands
Water – for drinking, cleaning
In the Community:
Petrol – for cars, motorcycles
Palm wine – for local ceremonies and drinks
Liquid soap – for cleaning

Importance of Specific Solids in Life

Why Solids are Important

Solids are equally useful because they:

Key Functions of Solids:
Provide shelter and tools – wood for building, iron for machines, cement for houses
Food – yam, maize, rice, groundnuts
Transport – car tyres (rubber), metals for car parts
Storage – plastic containers, glass bottles
Health – chalk (calcium carbonate), salt (sodium chloride)

Examples of Useful Solids

Salt → gives taste and helps preserve food

Salt is used in cooking to make food taste better. It also helps preserve food by preventing the growth of bacteria.

Charcoal → used for cooking

Charcoal is used as fuel for cooking, especially in areas where gas or electricity is not available.

Metals (iron, aluminium, copper) → used in construction and electricity

Iron is used for building structures and making tools. Aluminium is used for making pots, pans, and window frames. Copper is used for electrical wires.

Rubber → used for shoes, tyres, buckets

Rubber is flexible and waterproof, making it ideal for shoes, car tyres, and buckets.

Teaching Tip

Bring different solid objects to class and discuss their uses. Encourage students to think about how their lives would be different without these important solids.

The Periodic Table – Arrangement of Elements

What is the Periodic Table?

The periodic table is a scientific chart that shows all the elements in the world.

Key Facts
It has metals, non-metals, and noble gases
Elements are arranged in order of their atomic number (smallest particle number)
Elements in the same group (vertical column) have similar properties
Teaching Tip

Show students a large periodic table chart and point out the patterns. Explain how elements in the same group have similar properties and how the table is organized.

Types of Elements

Metals:
Sodium (Na), Magnesium (Mg), Aluminium (Al), Potassium (K), Calcium (Ca)

Metals are typically shiny, good conductors of heat and electricity, and can be hammered into thin sheets or drawn into wires.

Non-Metals:
Hydrogen (H), Carbon (C), Nitrogen (N), Oxygen (O), Sulphur (S), Phosphorus (P)

Non-metals are typically not shiny, poor conductors of heat and electricity, and cannot be hammered into sheets or drawn into wires.

Noble Gases (inactive gases):
Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar)

Noble gases are called "inactive" because they rarely react with other elements. They are used in lighting and balloons.

Teaching Tip

Use mnemonics to help students remember the first 20 elements. For example: "Happy Henry Likes Beer But Could Not Offer Full Nudges, Naughty Maggie Always Sings Poorly, Clumsy Arnold Kisses Cathy."

Quick Recall Summary

Key Points to Remember
States of Matter:
Solid = fixed shape and volume (stone, salt)
Liquid = fixed volume, no fixed shape (water, oil)
Gas = no fixed shape, no fixed volume (air, steam)
Importance of Liquids: drinking, cooking, transport, medicine, cleaning
Importance of Solids: building, food, machines, storage, health
Periodic Table:
First 20 elements → know their names and symbols
Metals (Na, Mg, Al), Non-metals (H, C, O), Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar)
Final Tip

Understanding the states of matter and the periodic table is fundamental to science. These concepts help us understand the world around us and how materials behave in different conditions.

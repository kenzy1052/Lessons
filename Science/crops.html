Sources of Plant Nutrients

Introduction to Plant Nutrients

Plants need nutrients to grow well, just like humans need food. Nutrients for plants come from two main sources:

Organic Nutrients

From natural sources

Inorganic Nutrients

Manufactured chemicals

Organic Nutrients

What are Organic Nutrients?

Organic nutrients come from living things. They are natural materials that provide essential elements for plant growth.

Examples of Organic Nutrients:
Animal manure – cow dung, poultry droppings, goat droppings
Compost – rotted leaves, kitchen waste, plant residues
Green manure – fresh plants buried in soil
Teaching Tip

Bring samples of different organic fertilizers to class for students to observe and compare. Discuss how each is made and its benefits for plants.

Inorganic Nutrients

What are Inorganic Nutrients?

Inorganic nutrients are manufactured in factories. They are chemical fertilizers that provide specific nutrients to plants.

Examples of Inorganic Nutrients:
NPK fertilizer
Ammonium sulphate
Urea
Potassium nitrate
Important Note

Inorganic fertilizers are concentrated and should be used carefully according to instructions. Overuse can harm plants and the environment.

Differences Between Organic and Inorganic Nutrients

Comparison Table

Feature Organic Nutrients Inorganic Nutrients
Source Natural (plants, animals, waste) Manufactured in factories
Nutrient release Slow, over a long time Quick, immediate
Cost Cheap, can be made locally Expensive, must be bought
Effect on soil Improves soil structure and fertility May damage soil if used too much
Example Manure, compost, green manure NPK, Urea, Ammonium sulphate

Advantages and Disadvantages

Organic Nutrients
Advantages:
  • Improves soil structure
  • Increases water-holding capacity
  • Releases nutrients slowly
  • Environmentally friendly
  • Can be made locally at low cost
Disadvantages:
  • Bulky and difficult to transport
  • Nutrient content is lower
  • Slower to show results
  • May contain weed seeds or pathogens
Inorganic Nutrients
Advantages:
  • High nutrient content
  • Quick results
  • Easy to transport and apply
  • Specific nutrient ratios available
Disadvantages:
  • Expensive
  • Can damage soil structure over time
  • May cause environmental pollution
  • Requires careful application

Nutrient Needs of Different Plants

Plant-Specific Nutrient Requirements

Different plants have different nutrient needs based on their growth patterns and the parts we harvest.

Vegetables

Need more nitrogen for leaf growth

Fruit Crops

Need more potassium for flowering and fruiting

Root/Tuber Crops

Need phosphorus for root development

Examples and Nutrient Requirements

Vegetables
Examples: lettuce, cabbage, tomatoes
Nutrient Need: High nitrogen (N) for leaf development
Reason: Leaves are the main harvested part, so plants need plenty of nitrogen to produce lush, green foliage
Fruit Crops
Examples: mango, orange, pawpaw
Nutrient Need: High potassium (K) for flowering and fruiting
Reason: Potassium helps with flower formation, fruit development, and disease resistance
Root/Tuber Crops
Examples: yam, cassava, carrots
Nutrient Need: High phosphorus (P) for root development
Reason: Phosphorus promotes strong root growth and helps plants store energy in their roots

Volume Considerations

Important Note

Sometimes organic fertilizers are bulky, so large amounts are needed to provide sufficient nutrients. Inorganic fertilizers are smaller in volume but richer in nutrients.

Volume Comparison
Organic: May need 5-10 kg of compost per square meter
Inorganic: May need only 100-200 grams of NPK per square meter
Consideration: While inorganic fertilizers require less volume, they must be applied more carefully to avoid over-fertilization

Physical Characteristics of Plant Nutrients

Organic Nutrients

Appearance
Look like rotted leaves, animal droppings, or soil-like compost
Bulky and dark brown/black in colour
Smell earthy or sometimes unpleasant
Texture and Structure
Compost: Crumbly texture, similar to rich garden soil
Animal manure: Can be powdery or fibrous depending on the animal and how it's processed
Green manure: Fresh plant material that is still recognizable as plant parts
Teaching Tip

Bring samples of different organic fertilizers for students to see, touch, and smell. This hands-on experience helps them remember the characteristics better.

Inorganic Nutrients

Appearance
Appear as granules, powder, or crystals
Light in weight but concentrated
Come in small colourful packs (e.g. white, blue, or pink granules)
Common Forms and Colors
Urea: White, round granules or crystals
NPK: Often blue or grey granules (may be multi-colored in blended formulas)
Ammonium sulphate: White crystals or granules
Potassium nitrate: White crystalline powder or granules
Safety Note

Inorganic fertilizers should be handled with care. Avoid inhaling dust, wash hands after use, and store away from children and animals.

Application of Plant Nutrients

Organic Fertilizers Application

Methods of Application
Spread on the soil and dug in: Manure and compost are spread evenly over the soil surface and then mixed into the soil with a hoe or tiller
Applied in heaps or ridges: Before planting, organic fertilizers can be placed in planting holes, heaps, or ridges where seeds or seedlings will be planted
Liquid manure: Some organic fertilizers can be soaked in water to create a nutrient-rich liquid that is poured near the base of plants
Best Practices
Apply organic fertilizers 2-4 weeks before planting to allow time for decomposition
Ensure organic fertilizers are well-composted to avoid introducing pathogens or burning plants
Mix thoroughly with soil to distribute nutrients evenly

Inorganic Fertilizers Application

Methods of Application
Broadcasting: Spreading fertilizer evenly over the entire soil surface, usually before planting or during early growth stages
Side dressing: Applying fertilizer beside growing plants, usually in a shallow trench or band a few inches from the plant stem
Ring method: Applying fertilizer in a ring around the base of a plant, especially for fruit trees or established plants
Foliar feeding: Spraying liquid fertilizer directly on leaves, where nutrients are absorbed quickly
Best Practices
Follow recommended application rates on the package
Apply when soil is moist to prevent burning plants
Avoid direct contact with plant stems and leaves (except for foliar feeding)
Water after application to help dissolve and distribute nutrients

Timing and Frequency

When to Apply Fertilizers
Organic: Apply before planting or during soil preparation. Can also be applied as a top dressing during the growing season
Inorganic: Apply at specific growth stages:
  • Basal application: Before or at planting
  • Top dressing: During active growth phases
  • Flowering/fruiting stage: To support flower and fruit development
Teaching Tip

Create a calendar showing when to apply different types of fertilizers for common crops. This helps students understand the importance of timing in fertilizer application.

Practical Work (School Garden Activities)

Activity 1: Identifying Nutrient Sources

1
Objective: Identify nutrient sources available in your community
2
Materials needed: Notebook, pen, camera (optional), sample bags
3
Procedure:
  • Walk around the school and nearby community
  • Look for examples of organic and inorganic fertilizers
  • Record what you find (name, source, appearance)
  • Take photos if possible
  • Collect small samples if appropriate and safe
4
Examples to look for: Poultry manure, compost heap, NPK fertilizer, animal droppings, kitchen waste composting area

Activity 2: Comparing Fertilizers

1
Objective: Compare organic vs inorganic fertilizers
2
Materials needed: Samples of organic and inorganic fertilizers, observation table, magnifying glass
3
Procedure:
  • Observe the physical characteristics of each fertilizer
  • Record color, texture, smell, and form
  • Compare the cost (if information is available)
  • Discuss the effects on soil and plants
4
Create a comparison table with the following headings: Type, Appearance, Smell, Cost, Effect on Soil, Advantages, Disadvantages

Activity 3: Applying Fertilizers

1
Objective: Practice applying both types of fertilizers in the school garden
2
Materials needed: Organic fertilizer (compost or manure), inorganic fertilizer (NPK), garden tools, watering can, measuring tools
3
Procedure:
  • Prepare garden beds for planting
  • Apply organic fertilizer to one bed using the digging-in method
  • Apply inorganic fertilizer to another bed using broadcasting
  • Plant the same type of seeds in both beds
  • Water both beds equally
  • Observe and record plant growth over time
4
Record observations: Note differences in germination rate, growth speed, plant health, and yield

Safety Considerations

Safety First
  • Always wash hands after handling fertilizers
  • Wear gloves when handling inorganic fertilizers
  • Avoid inhaling dust from fertilizers
  • Do not apply fertilizers on windy days
  • Store fertilizers in a safe, dry place away from children
  • Follow recommended application rates

Quick Recall Summary

Key Points to Remember
Sources of Plant Nutrients:
Organic → from living things (manure, compost, green manure)
Inorganic → manufactured chemicals (NPK, Urea, Ammonium sulphate)
Differences:
Organic → slow release, improves soil, cheaper, bulky
Inorganic → quick results, may damage soil, expensive, concentrated
Nutrient Needs:
Vegetables → nitrogen for leaves
Fruit crops → potassium for flowering and fruiting
Root/tuber crops → phosphorus for root development
Application Methods:
Organic → digging in, heaps/ridges, liquid manure
Inorganic → broadcasting, side dressing, ring method, foliar feeding
Final Tip

Understanding plant nutrients is essential for successful crop production. Both organic and inorganic fertilizers have their place in agriculture. The best approach often involves using both types in a balanced way to maintain soil health while providing plants with the nutrients they need.

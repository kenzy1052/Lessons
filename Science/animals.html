Introduction to Domestic Animals

What are Domestic Animals?

Definition

Domestic animals are animals that have been tamed and kept by humans for different purposes such as food, work, and companionship.

Examples of Domestic Animals
Goats
Sheep
Cattle
Pigs
Rabbits
Chickens
Ducks
Turkeys

Why Domestic Animals Matter

Importance to Humans
Food Source: Provide meat, milk, eggs, and other food products
Labour: Help with farming tasks like ploughing and transportation
Income: Can be sold for money or their products can be sold
Companionship: Some animals are kept as pets for emotional support
By-products: Provide materials like leather, wool, and manure for fertilizer
Teaching Tip

Show students pictures or videos of different domestic animals and discuss their importance in daily life. Ask students to share their experiences with domestic animals.

Characteristics Used to Identify Domestic Animals

Key Identifying Features

Shape & Size
Animals vary greatly in size and shape, which helps in identification
Example: Cattle are bigger than goats
Example: Chickens are smaller than turkeys
Colour
Coat or feather color is a common identifying feature
Examples: White, black, brown, spotted
Example: Some cattle are white with black spots (Holstein)
Food/Feeding Habit
What an animal eats helps classify it
Herbivores: Eat only plants (cattle, goats, sheep)
Omnivores: Eat both plants and meat (pigs, dogs, chickens)
Uses
The purpose for which an animal is kept helps identify its type
Examples: Meat, milk, eggs, labour
Example: Dairy cattle are kept mainly for milk production

Classification of Domestic Animals

Main Classes of Domestic Animals

Domestic animals can be grouped into three main classes based on their digestive systems and other characteristics:

Ruminants

Four-compartment stomach

Monogastrics

Single-compartment stomach

Poultry

Birds with beaks

1. Ruminants

What are Ruminants?

Animals with a stomach divided into four compartments. They chew food, swallow, bring it back (cud), and chew again.

Examples of Ruminants
Cattle
Sheep
Goats
Characteristics of Ruminants
Have horns (mostly)
Chew cud
Produce milk (in females)
Strong and can be used for work (e.g., ploughing)
Feeding Habit
Herbivores - eat only plants
Can digest tough plant fibers thanks to their specialized digestive system

2. Monogastrics

What are Monogastrics?

Animals with a simple (single) stomach. They cannot chew cud.

Examples of Monogastrics
Pigs
Rabbits
Dogs
Characteristics of Monogastrics
Digest food once
Grow fast
Eat different types of food (depending on the species)
Feeding Habit
Omnivores (pigs, dogs) - eat plants and meat
Herbivores (rabbits) - eat only plants

3. Poultry

What is Poultry?

Monogastric herbivores with beaks. Domesticated birds kept for meat, eggs, and feathers.

Examples of Poultry
Chickens
Turkeys
Ducks
Guinea fowls
Characteristics of Poultry
Have beaks instead of teeth
Lay eggs
Covered with feathers
Lightweight bones for easy movement
Feeding Habit
Mostly eat grains, seeds, and plants
Use gizzard to grind food since they don't have teeth

Differences Between Ruminants, Monogastrics and Poultry

Comparative Features

Feature Ruminants Monogastrics Poultry
Stomach Four compartments One compartment One compartment
Examples Cattle, sheep, goats Pig, rabbit, dog Chicken, duck, turkey
Feeding habit Herbivores Omnivores/herbivores Mostly grains, plants
Food digestion Chew cud No cud chewing Use gizzard to grind
Body covering Hair/fur Hair/fur Feathers
Reproduction Give birth (viviparous) Give birth (viviparous) Lay eggs (oviparous)

Digestive System Differences

Ruminant Digestive System
Four compartments: rumen, reticulum, omasum, abomasum
Can digest cellulose (plant fiber) efficiently
Process: Chew → Swallow → Regurgitate as cud → Chew again → Digest
Monogastric Digestive System
Simple stomach with one compartment
Cannot digest cellulose as efficiently as ruminants
Process: Chew → Swallow → Digest (single pass)
Poultry Digestive System
Simple stomach with one compartment
Have a gizzard that grinds food (compensates for lack of teeth)
Process: Swallow whole → Store in crop → Grind in gizzard → Digest

Similarities Among Ruminants, Monogastrics and Poultry

Common Features

Domestication
All are domesticated by humans for various purposes
Have been selectively bred for specific traits over many generations
Food Production
All are used for food (meat, eggs, milk)
Form an important part of the human food chain
Basic Needs
All need feeding, water, and shelter to survive and thrive
Require proper care and management for optimal health and productivity
Economic Importance
All can be reared for commercial or domestic purposes
Contribute to the economy through agriculture and food production

Uses of Domestic Animals

1. Domestic Uses

Food
Meat: Beef from cattle, pork from pigs, mutton from sheep, chevon from goats
Milk: From cattle, goats, and sheep
Eggs: From chickens, ducks, and turkeys
Clothing
Hides and skins: Used for leather products like shoes, bags, belts
Wool: From sheep, used for sweaters, blankets, and other warm clothing
Feathers: From poultry, used for pillows, decorations, and dusters
Work
Ploughing: Oxen (castrated bulls) are used to plough fields
Transportation: Donkeys, horses, and cattle are used for riding and carrying loads
Hunting: Dogs are used for hunting game
Companionship
Pets: Dogs, cats, and rabbits provide companionship and emotional support
Therapy animals: Some animals are trained to help people with disabilities or provide emotional support
Manure
Animal droppings are used to fertilize crops
Provides organic matter that improves soil structure and fertility

2. Commercial Uses

Sale for Money
Meat: Sold in markets and butcheries
Milk: Sold fresh or processed into cheese, yogurt, butter
Eggs: Sold for consumption or hatching
Hides and skins: Sold to tanneries for leather production
Wool: Sold to textile industries
Commercial Farming
Poultry farming: Raising chickens for eggs (layers) or meat (broilers)
Piggeries: Raising pigs for pork production
Dairy farming: Raising cattle for milk production
Sheep and goat farming: For meat, milk, and fiber production
Breeding
Breeding stock is sold to other farmers
Selective breeding for improved traits is a valuable commercial activity

Quick Recall Summary

Key Points to Remember
Domestic animals: Tamed by humans for food, work, and companionship
Characteristics for identification: Shape & size, colour, feeding habit, uses
Classification:
Ruminants → Four stomach compartments, chew cud, herbivores
Monogastrics → Simple stomach, no cud chewing, omnivores/herbivores
Poultry → Birds with beaks, lay eggs, use gizzard to grind food
Differences: Stomach structure, feeding habits, digestion process, body covering, reproduction
Similarities: All domesticated, used for food, need care, economic importance
Uses:
Domestic → Food, clothing, work, companionship, manure
Commercial → Sold for money, farming enterprises, breeding
By-products: Milk, hides, wool, manure, eggs, feathers, lard, bristles
Final Tip

Understanding domestic animals and their classification is essential for successful animal production. Each type has specific needs and characteristics that farmers must understand to provide proper care and maximize productivity.

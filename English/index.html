B7.3.1.1.1: Nouns - Understanding and Application

What are Nouns?

Nouns are words that name people, places, things, or ideas. They are the building blocks of sentences and help us communicate about the world around us.

Key Definition

Noun: A word that identifies a person, place, thing, or idea.

Types of Nouns

There are several types of nouns that students need to recognize and use correctly:

1. Common Nouns

Common nouns name general items rather than specific ones. They are not capitalized unless they begin a sentence.

teacher, school, book, city, country
Example: The teacher explained the lesson to the class.
2. Proper Nouns

Proper nouns name specific people, places, or things. They are always capitalized.

Mr. Johnson, Ghana, Accra, Bible, January
Example: Mrs. Adu teaches at Presby Senior High School.
3. Countable Nouns

Countable nouns are things we can count. They have both singular and plural forms.

book/books, student/students, chair/chairs
Example: There are thirty students in the classroom.
4. Uncountable Nouns

Uncountable nouns are things we cannot count separately. They usually don't have a plural form.

water, rice, information, furniture, advice
Example: Please pass me some water. (NOT: Please pass me a water.)
5. Concrete Nouns

Concrete nouns are things we can see, touch, hear, smell, or taste.

table, dog, music, apple, perfume
Example: The dog barked at the mailman.

Plural Noun Forms

Most nouns form their plurals by adding -s or -es, but some have irregular plural forms:

Regular Plurals
Add -s: book → books, pen → pens
Add -es: box → boxes, bus → buses
Change -y to -i and add -es: baby → babies, city → cities
Irregular Plurals
mouse → mice
child → children
sheep → sheep (no change)
half → halves
man → men
woman → women
person → people
tooth → teeth
foot → feet
Teaching Tip

Create flashcards with irregular plurals and play matching games. Use real objects when possible to help students visualize the difference between singular and plural forms.

Using Nouns in Sentences

Here are examples of sentences using different types of nouns:

Examples:
Common nouns: The student borrowed a book from the library.
Proper nouns: Kofi visited Cape Coast Castle during his Easter holiday.
Countable nouns: She bought three apples and two oranges at the market.
Uncountable nouns: We need some furniture for the new office.
Concrete nouns: The rain made the streets wet and slippery.
Practice Activity

Identify the type of noun in bold in this sentence: "The children visited the museum in Accra."

B7.3.1.1.2: Pronouns - Types and Usage

What are Pronouns?

Pronouns are words that take the place of nouns or noun phrases. They help avoid repetition and make sentences flow more naturally.

Key Definition

Pronoun: A word that replaces or refers to a noun or noun phrase.

Example:

Without pronoun: Ama lost Ama's book, but Ama found Ama's book later.

With pronouns: Ama lost her book, but she found it later.

Types of Pronouns

1. Personal Pronouns

Personal pronouns refer to specific people or things. They change form based on their function in the sentence (subject or object).

Subject Object Possessive
I, you, he, she, it, we, they me, you, him, her, it, us, them my, your, his, her, its, our, their
She gave me her notes.
2. Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns show ownership or possession.

mine, yours, his, hers, its, ours, theirs
This book is mine, not yours.
3. Relative Pronouns

Relative pronouns connect a dependent clause to a noun or pronoun.

who, whom, whose, which, that
The student who won the prize is my friend.
This is the book that I told you about.
4. Reflexive Pronouns

Reflexive pronouns refer back to the subject of the sentence.

myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves
I taught myself to play the guitar.
She hurt herself while playing.
5. Interrogative Pronouns

Interrogative pronouns are used to ask questions.

who, whom, whose, which, what
Who is coming to the party?
Whose book is this?
6. Demonstrative Pronouns

Demonstrative pronouns point to specific things.

this, that, these, those
This is my favorite book.
Those are the students who won the competition.

Using Pronouns in Context

Examples in Sentences:
Personal: They are going to the market with us.
Possessive: The blue car is hers, but the red one is ours.
Relative: The teacher who taught me last year has moved to another school.
Reflexive: The children cleaned the classroom themselves.
Interrogative: What did you learn in school today?
Demonstrative: This is the way to solve the problem.
Student Tip

Remember that pronouns must agree with the nouns they replace in number (singular/plural) and gender (masculine/feminine/neuter).

Practice Activity

Replace the underlined words with the correct pronoun: "Kofi and Ama went to the market, and Kofi and Ama bought fruits."

B7.3.1.1.3: Adjectives - Description and Modification

What are Adjectives?

Adjectives are words that describe or modify nouns and pronouns. They give more information about the qualities, states, or attributes of the things we talk about.

Key Definition

Adjective: A word that describes or modifies a noun or pronoun.

Example:

The happy children played in the beautiful garden with colorful balls.

Position of Adjectives

1. Directly Before Nouns

Adjectives usually come directly before the nouns they modify.

Asiedu is an excellent singer.
She bought a beautiful dress.
They live in a modern house.
2. With the Verb "to be"

Adjectives can come after the verb "to be" to describe the subject of the sentence.

Adzo was very tired.
The weather is pleasant today.
They were excited about the trip.
3. With Sense Verbs or Verbs of Appearance

Adjectives can come after sense verbs (sight, taste, smell, touch, hearing) or verbs of appearance to modify the nouns that come before the verb.

The fish tasted awful.
He seemed very upset.
The flowers smell wonderful.
The music sounds loud.
The blanket feels soft.

Types of Adjectives

1. Descriptive Adjectives

These describe qualities or characteristics.

Beautiful sunset, intelligent student, difficult problem
2. Quantitative Adjectives

These indicate quantity or number.

Three books, many friends, some water
3. Demonstrative Adjectives

These point out specific things.

This book, those students, that idea
4. Possessive Adjectives

These show ownership or possession.

My book, her car, their house

Using Adjectives Effectively

Examples in Context:
The brave firefighter rescued the frightened cat from the tall tree.
After the long journey, the exhausted travelers finally reached their comfortable hotel.
The delicious food smelled wonderful and tasted even better.
Teaching Tip

Encourage students to use specific, vivid adjectives rather than general ones. Instead of "good," encourage "excellent," "outstanding," "superb," etc.

Practice Activity

Add appropriate adjectives to this sentence: "The _____ children played in the _____ garden with _____ balls."

B7.3.1.1.4: Verbs - Tenses and Aspects

What are Verbs?

Verbs are words that express action, occurrence, or state of being. They are essential for constructing meaningful sentences and indicate when something happens (tense).

Key Definition

Verb: A word that expresses action, occurrence, or state of being.

Tense: The form of a verb that shows the time when an action happens.

Aspect: The form of a verb that shows the nature of the action (completed, ongoing, etc.).

Present Tense

1. Simple Present Tense

Used for habitual actions, general truths, and permanent situations.

I wash a car. (habitual action)
The sun rises in the east. (general truth)
She lives in Accra. (permanent situation)
2. Present Progressive (Continuous)

Used for actions happening now, temporary situations, and future arrangements.

I am washing a car. (action happening now)
She is staying with her aunt this week. (temporary situation)
We are meeting tomorrow. (future arrangement)
3. Present Perfect

Used for actions completed at an unspecified time in the past, actions that started in the past and continue to the present, and past actions with present results.

I have washed the car. (completed action)
She has lived here for ten years. (continues to present)
They have broken the window. (present result)
4. Present Perfect Progressive

Used for actions that started in the past, continue to the present, and may continue into the future, with emphasis on duration.

I have been washing the car for an hour. (emphasizes duration)
She has been studying English since primary school. (continues to present)

Past Tense

1. Simple Past Tense

Used for completed actions at a specific time in the past.

I washed the car yesterday.
She visited her grandmother last week.
They arrived at 6 PM.
2. Past Progressive (Continuous)

Used for actions in progress at a specific time in the past, interrupted actions, and background actions in stories.

I was washing the car when it started to rain. (interrupted action)
She was sleeping at 10 PM last night. (action in progress)
The sun was shining and birds were singing. (background actions)
3. Past Perfect

Used for actions completed before another past action, and to emphasize that an action was completed before a specific time in the past.

I had washed the car before the storm started. (completed before another action)
She had already left when I arrived. (completed before another action)
By 5 PM, they had finished the project. (completed before a specific time)
4. Past Perfect Progressive

Used for actions that were in progress before another past action, with emphasis on duration.

I had been washing the car for an hour when the rain started. (emphasizes duration)
She had been studying for hours before the exam. (emphasizes duration)

Using Verbs in Context

Examples in Context:
Simple Present: Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius.
Present Progressive: The children are playing in the garden right now.
Present Perfect: I have visited Cape Coast Castle twice.
Present Perfect Progressive: She has been working on this project for months.
Simple Past: We watched a movie last night.
Past Progressive: They were having dinner when I called.
Past Perfect: The train had already left when we reached the station.
Past Perfect Progressive: He had been waiting for an hour before the bus arrived.
Student Tip

Remember that verb tenses help us understand when actions happen. Pay attention to time markers like "yesterday," "now," "since," "for," "already," etc., as they often indicate which tense to use.

Practice Activity

Complete this sentence with the correct form of the verb "study": "She _____ for three hours before the exam started."

B7.3.1.1.5: Adverbs - Modification and Description

What are Adverbs?

Adverbs are words that modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. They provide more information about how, when, where, or to what extent something happens.

Key Definition

Adverb: A word that modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb.

Example:

She quickly ran to the store. (modifies verb "ran")

The test was extremely difficult. (modifies adjective "difficult")

The cheetah runs incredibly quickly. (modifies adverb "quickly")

Types of Adverbs

1. Adverbs of Manner

These describe how an action is performed. They often end in -ly.

She slowly entered the room.
He carefully solved the problem.
They happily celebrated their victory.
2. Adverbs of Time

These indicate when an action happens.

She will arrive tomorrow.
They already finished their homework.
He recently moved to a new city.
3. Adverbs of Place

These indicate where an action happens.

The children are playing outside.
She looked everywhere for her keys.
They went abroad for vacation.
4. Adverbs of Frequency

These indicate how often an action happens.

She always arrives on time.
They sometimes visit their grandparents.
He rarely eats fast food.
5. Adverbs of Degree

These indicate the extent or degree of an action or quality.

The test was extremely difficult.
She was very happy with the results.
He is almost finished with his project.

Using Adverbs to Modify

1. Modifying Verbs

Adverbs give more information about the verb they modify.

She slowly entered the room.
The children happily played in the garden.
He carefully crossed the street.
2. Modifying Adjectives

Adverbs give more information about the adjective they modify.

The test was extremely difficult.
She has a remarkably beautiful voice.
It was a surprisingly easy task.
3. Modifying Other Adverbs

The first adverb gives more information about the second adverb.

The cheetah runs incredibly quickly.
She completed the task very efficiently.
He speaks quite fluently.

Using Adverbs Effectively

Examples in Context:
The students diligently worked on their assignments and successfully completed them before the deadline.
The extremely talented musician performed beautifully at the concert last night.
She always arrives punctually for her classes and consistently produces high-quality work.
Teaching Tip

Help students recognize that not all adverbs end in -ly. Words like "very," "quite," "too," and "almost" are also adverbs. Also, teach students that adverbs can appear in different positions in a sentence.

Practice Activity

Add an appropriate adverb to this sentence: "The student answered the question _____."

B7.3.1.1.6: Conjunctions - Connecting Ideas

What are Conjunctions?

Conjunctions are words that connect words, phrases, or clauses. They help create more complex and interesting sentences by showing relationships between ideas.

Key Definition

Conjunction: A word that connects words, phrases, or clauses.

Example:

I wanted to go to the market, but it started raining.

She likes tea and coffee.

Because she studied hard, she passed the exam.

Types of Conjunctions

1. Coordinating Conjunctions

These connect words, phrases, or independent clauses of equal importance. Remember the acronym FANBOYS:

For - shows reason: She was tired, for she had worked all day.
And - shows addition: She bought apples and oranges.
Nor - shows negative choice: He doesn't like coffee, nor does he like tea.
But - shows contrast: She is smart, but sometimes makes mistakes.
Or - shows choice: You can study now, or you can play outside.
Yet - shows contrast: The movie was long, yet it was interesting.
So - shows result: It was raining, so we stayed indoors.
2. Correlative Conjunctions

These work in pairs to connect grammatically equal elements.

Either... or: You can either study now or play later.
Neither... nor: She neither called nor texted me.
Not only... but also: He is not only intelligent but also hardworking.
Both... and: Both teachers and students attended the event.
Whether... or: I don't know whether she will come or not.
3. Subordinating Conjunctions

These connect a dependent clause to an independent clause, showing the relationship between them.

Because: Because it was raining, we stayed home.
Since: Since you're here, let's start the meeting.
As: As I was leaving, the phone rang.
Although: Although it was expensive, she bought it.
Though: Though he was tired, he finished his work.
While: She read a book while waiting for the bus.
Whereas: He is very outgoing, whereas his brother is shy.
4. Adverbs that Function as Conjunctions

These adverbial conjunctions connect independent clauses.

Until: Wait here until I return.
After: After the movie ended, we went home.
Before: Finish your homework before you go out to play.

Using Conjunctions Effectively

Examples in Context:
Coordinating: She wanted to join the club, but she didn't have enough time.
Correlative: Not only did she win the race, but also broke the record.
Subordinating: Although it was raining, the children continued playing outside.
Adverbial Conjunction: We'll wait here until the bus arrives.
Student Tip

Remember that when using coordinating conjunctions (FANBOYS) to connect two independent clauses, you need a comma before the conjunction. Example: "I wanted to go, but it was too late."

Practice Activity

Combine these two sentences using an appropriate conjunction: "She studied hard. She passed the exam."

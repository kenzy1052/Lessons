Example 1: Personal Narrative

I went to the market. I bought fruits. I saw my friend. I went home.
Improved: Last Saturday, I went to the market with my mother. First, we bought fresh fruits. Then, I saw my friend, who was very happy to see me. Finally, I went home feeling excited about our meeting.

Example 2: Descriptive Writing

The man walked in the room. His clothes were dirty. The room was dark.
Improved: The tall man staggered into the room. His clothes were torn and muddy, and a strong smell of smoke filled the dimly lit room.

Example 3: Advertisement

Buy our soap. It is good. It cleans clothes. It is cheap.
Improved: Amazing Bright Soap! Do you want clean clothes every day? Buy Bright Soap today. It is cheap, fast, and effective. Try it now and see the difference!

Example 4: Process Writing

How to make tea. Get water. Boil it. Put tea in cup. Pour water. Add milk.
Improved: How to Make Tea First, get some clean water. Next, boil the water until it bubbles. Then, put a tea bag in your cup. After that, pour the hot water over the tea bag. Finally, add milk or sugar to taste.

Example 5: Dialogue Writing

Kofi: Where are you going? Ama: I am going to the market. Kofi: Can I come? Ama: Yes.
Improved: Kofi: "Where are you going in such a hurry?"
Ama: "I'm going to the market to buy some vegetables for dinner."
Kofi: "That sounds interesting! Can I come with you?"
Ama: "Of course! I'd love the company."

Narrative and Descriptive Writing

1. Personal Narratives (B7.4.2.1.1)

What is a Personal Narrative?

A personal narrative is a story about your own life or experience.

Features of Personal Narratives
Written in first person (I, me, my, we, our).
Uses descriptive details (setting, events, feelings).
Follows a logical order (beginning ‚Üí middle ‚Üí end).
Uses grammar correctly:
  • Adjectives ‚Äì big, beautiful, red, etc.
  • Adverbs ‚Äì slowly, quickly, happily.
  • Connectors ‚Äì first, then, afterwards, finally.
  • Direct/indirect speech ‚Äì "I said‚Ä¶", He told me‚Ä¶
  • Prepositions ‚Äì in, on, at, near, with.
Example of a Personal Narrative
"Last Saturday, I went to the market with my mother. First, we bought fresh vegetables. Then, we saw my aunt. I was so happy because she gave me money."

2. Descriptive Writing (B7.4.2.1.2)

What is Descriptive Writing?

Descriptive writing creates a clear picture of a person, place, or event.

Features of Descriptive Writing
Uses precise words (not just "walked" but "staggered," "marched," "tiptoed").
Uses sensory language (words for sight, sound, smell, taste, touch).
Uses grammar correctly: adjectives, adverbs, connectors, prepositions, direct speech.
Example of Descriptive Writing
"The tall man staggered into the room. His clothes were torn, and a strong smell of smoke filled the air."
Practice Activity

Transform this simple description into a more vivid one:

Original: "The girl walked to school. She was happy. The sun was shining."
Improved: "The young girl skipped happily to school, with a bright smile on her face. The warm morning sun cast golden rays on the path ahead, making everything seem magical."

3. Classroom Tips

Teaching Tips
  • Use Sensory Prompts: Give students objects to touch, taste, smell, or hear before writing descriptions.
  • Memory Lane: Ask students to close their eyes and recall a memorable event before writing personal narratives.
  • Show, Don't Tell: Teach students to describe actions and feelings rather than just stating them.
  • Word Banks: Create lists of strong verbs, adjectives, and adverbs for students to use in their writing.
  • Peer Review: Have students read each other's work and suggest improvements.

Advertisements and Process Writing

1. Advertisements (B7.4.2.1.3)

What is an Advertisement?

An advertisement is a short message to persuade people to buy a product or service.

Features of Advertisements
Has a title (catchy).
Body ‚Äì information about the product/service.
Target audience ‚Äì who is it for?
Selling points ‚Äì why is it good?
Uses language techniques:
  • Simple present tense (This soap cleans fast.)
  • Modals/commands (Buy now! You must try this!)
  • Rhetorical questions (Are you tired of dirty clothes?)
  • Repetition (Fresh! Fresh! Fresh!)
Example of an Advertisement
Amazing Bright Soap!
Do you want clean clothes every day? Buy Bright Soap today. It is cheap, fast, and effective. Try it now and see the difference!

2. Process/Expository Writing (B7.4.2.1.4)

What is Process Writing?

Process writing explains how something is done or how something works.

Features of Process Writing
State the aim.
List materials needed.
Give steps in the correct order.
Use connectors: first, next, then, finally.
Use simple present tense.
Example: How to Brush Your Teeth
First, take your toothbrush and toothpaste.
Next, put toothpaste on the brush.
Then, brush your teeth gently up and down.
After that, rinse your mouth with water.
Finally, clean your toothbrush.
Practice Activity

Write a process paragraph for one of these activities:

How to make a sandwich
How to plant a seed
How to send an email

3. Classroom Tips

Teaching Tips
  • Analyze Real Ads: Bring in real advertisements and have students identify the persuasive techniques used.
  • Create a Product: Have students invent a product and create an advertisement for it.
  • Step-by-Step: For process writing, have students first perform the task, then write down the steps.
  • Sequence Cards: Create cards with steps of a process mixed up, and have students arrange them in order.
  • Peer Evaluation: Have students evaluate each other's advertisements and process instructions for clarity.

Letters and Notes-Taking

1. Informal Letters (B7.4.2.2.1)

What are Informal Letters?

Letters written to friends or family.

Features of Informal Letters
Writer's address & date (top right).
Greeting/Salutation (Dear Ama,).
Body ‚Äì main message.
Closing (Yours sincerely/Your friend,).
Name.
Example Opening of an Informal Letter
"Dear Kofi,
How are you? I hope you are fine. I want to tell you about my new school..."

2. Formal Letters (B7.4.2.2.2)

What are Formal Letters?

Letters written to officials or institutions.

Features of Formal Letters
Writer's address & date.
Recipient's address.
Heading/Subject.
Salutation (Dear Sir/Madam,).
Body (polite and straight to the point).
Closing (Yours faithfully,).
Signature & name.

3. Notes-Taking (B7.4.2.2.3)

What is Notes-Taking?

Taking notes means writing short, clear points from a lesson, text, or speech.

Tips for Notes-Taking
Write headings.
Use bullet points.
Record key ideas only (not full sentences).
Use drawings or symbols if needed.
Example of Notes
Causes of Flooding
‚Ä¢ Heavy rain
‚Ä¢ Choked gutters
‚Ä¢ Building in water areas

4. Classroom Tips

Teaching Tips
  • Letter Templates: Provide templates for both formal and informal letters to help students understand the structure.
  • Real Contexts: Have students write letters for real purposes (e.g., to invite a guest speaker, to request information).
  • Listening Practice: Play short audio clips or give short talks and have students take notes.
  • Compare Notes: After note-taking activities, have students compare their notes with a partner.
  • Letter Exchange: Organize a pen pal system where students write and receive letters.

Articles and Dialogue Writing

1. Notices and Posters (B7.4.2.2.4)

What are Notices and Posters?

Used to give information to a group.

Features of Notices and Posters
Short and clear.
Has a heading.
Uses simple present tense.
May include drawings/pictures.
Example of a Notice
NOTICE
There will be a clean-up exercise on Saturday, 7:00 a.m. All students must be present.

2. Article Writing (B7.4.2.2.5)

What is an Article?

Articles are written for publication (school magazine, newspaper, blog).

Features of Articles
Title/Heading.
Introduction (topic).
Body (main points, arguments).
Conclusion (summary, call to action).

3. Dialogue Writing (B7.4.2.2.6)

What is Dialogue?

Dialogue is a conversation between two or more people.

Features of Dialogue
Use quotation marks (" ").
Each speaker gets a new line.
Keep it short and clear.
Example of Dialogue
Ama: "Where are you going?"
Kojo: "I'm going to the library. Do you want to join me?"

4. Classroom Tips

Teaching Tips
  • School Newspaper: Create a class or school newspaper where students can contribute articles.
  • Role Play: Have students act out dialogues to practice natural conversation.
  • Notice Board: Create a classroom notice board where students can post their own notices.
  • Interview Practice: Have students interview each other and write articles based on the interviews.
  • Dialogue Completion: Provide incomplete dialogues for students to finish.

5. Key Reminder

Remember

Good writing should be clear, logical, and appropriate for the audience and purpose. Always edit and proofread your work.

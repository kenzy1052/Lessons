Using Cohesive Devices to Link Sentences and Paragraphs

1. Cohesion in Writing

What is Cohesion?

Cohesion means joining ideas, sentences, or paragraphs so that your writing flows smoothly and makes sense.

We use cohesive devices such as:

  • Conjunctions (connectives)
  • Pronouns
  • Punctuation
  • Repetition of words, synonyms, and antonyms

2. Conjunctions

A. Compound Sentences (joined with coordinating conjunctions)

Conjunctions: nor, or, so, then, yet

Example: "I was tired, yet I finished my homework."
Example: "You can eat rice, or you can eat yam."
B. Complex Sentences (joined with subordinating conjunctions)
Time clauses: when, before, after, since, while, as, until
Example: "We waited until the rain stopped."
Conditional clauses: if, unless
Example: "You will fail if you don't learn."
Purpose clauses: in order to, so that
Example: "She studied hard so that she could pass."
Reason clauses: because, since, as
Example: "He left early because he was sick."
Result clauses: so that
Example: "It rained heavily so that the streets were flooded."
Concessive clauses: although, though, while
Example: "Although it was late, she continued reading."
Place clauses: where, wherever
Example: "Sit where you feel comfortable."
Manner clauses: as, like, the way
Example: "Do it the way your teacher showed you."

3. Pronouns

Types of Pronouns

Pronouns replace nouns so we don't repeat words too much.

Subject pronouns: he, she, they, we
Object pronouns: me, him, them
Demonstrative pronouns: this, that, these, those
Example: "Kofi is a good boy. He helps his mother."
Example: "I met Ama. I spoke to her yesterday."

4. Punctuation

Punctuation Marks as Cohesive Devices

Punctuation marks help us organize ideas in writing.

Full stop (.) – ends a statement
Comma (,) – separates ideas in a sentence
Question mark (?) – shows a question
Exclamation mark (!) – shows surprise or strong feeling
Quotation marks (" ") – show direct speech
Example: "She asked, 'Where are you going?'"
Example: "Wow! That is amazing."

5. Logical Connectors

Types of Logical Connectors

These are words/phrases that link ideas.

Ordering: firstly, secondly, finally
Addition: moreover, furthermore, in addition
Similarity: similarly, likewise
Contrast: however, although, on the other hand
Cause/Effect: because, therefore, as a result
Time/Sequence: next, soon, later, afterwards

6. Practice Activity for Students

A. Combine these sentences using appropriate conjunctions:
Original: "She was hungry. She couldn't eat."
Possible answers:
  • She was hungry, but she couldn't eat.
  • Although she was hungry, she couldn't eat.
  • She was hungry, yet she couldn't eat.
B. Replace repeated nouns with pronouns:
Original: "Ama went to the market. Ama bought fruits. Ama gave the fruits to Kofi."
Corrected: "Ama went to the market. She bought fruits. She gave them to Kofi."
C. Add logical connectors to these sentences:
Original: "I woke up early. I ate breakfast. I went to school."
Corrected: "First, I woke up early. Then, I ate breakfast. Afterwards, I went to school."

7. Classroom Tips

Teaching Tips
  • Visual Aids: Create posters with different types of cohesive devices and examples.
  • Group Activities: Have students work in pairs to connect unrelated sentences using appropriate cohesive devices.
  • Real-world Examples: Use newspaper articles or short stories to identify cohesive devices in authentic texts.
  • Games: Play "sentence chain" where each student adds a sentence to a story using a cohesive device.
  • Writing Practice: Assign short writing tasks focusing on using specific types of cohesive devices.

8. Recap of Key Rules

Cohesive Devices
  • Conjunctions join sentences and show relationships between ideas
  • Pronouns replace nouns to avoid repetition
  • Punctuation helps organize ideas and improve readability
  • Logical connectors show the relationship between ideas
Remember

Good writing is clear, connected, and smooth. Use conjunctions, pronouns, punctuation, and connectors to make your writing beautiful.

Writing a Good Paragraph

1. What is a Paragraph?

Definition

A paragraph is a group of sentences about one main idea.

A well-written paragraph has three main parts:

  • Topic sentence – introduces the main idea
  • Supporting sentences – explain or give examples
  • Concluding sentence – may summarize or end the idea

2. Steps to Writing a Good Paragraph

Step 1: Topic Sentence

The topic sentence introduces the main idea of the paragraph. It is usually the first sentence.

Example: "Yesterday was exciting."
This sentence tells the reader that the paragraph will be about an exciting day.
Step 2: Supporting Sentences

These sentences give details, examples, or explanations about the main idea.

Example: "First, I woke up early. Then, I went to school with my friends. Afterwards, we played football."
These sentences explain what made the day exciting.
Step 3: Concluding Sentence

This sentence wraps up the paragraph and may express a feeling or summarize the main idea.

Example: "Finally, I returned home tired but happy."
This sentence concludes the paragraph by describing the feeling at the end of the day.

3. Example of a Complete Paragraph

Narrative Paragraph
Topic sentence: Yesterday was exciting.
Supporting sentences: First, I woke up early. Then, I went to school with my friends. Afterwards, we played football.
Concluding sentence: Finally, I returned home tired but happy.
Complete paragraph: Yesterday was exciting. First, I woke up early. Then, I went to school with my friends. Afterwards, we played football. Finally, I returned home tired but happy.

4. Types of Paragraphs

A. Narrative Paragraph

Tells a story or recounts events in chronological order.

Example: "Last weekend, my family and I went to the beach. We arrived early in the morning and set up our umbrella. My brother and I swam in the sea while our parents prepared lunch. In the afternoon, we built sandcastles and collected shells. We left when the sun began to set, feeling happy and relaxed."
B. Descriptive Paragraph

Describes a person, place, or thing in detail.

Example: "My bedroom is my favorite place in the house. The walls are painted a calming blue color, and my bed has a soft, white comforter. In one corner, I have a small bookshelf filled with my favorite books. A large window lets in plenty of sunlight during the day, and at night, I can see the stars from my bed. It's a peaceful space where I can relax and think."
C. Expository Paragraph

Explains or informs about a topic.

Example: "Photosynthesis is the process by which plants make their own food. It occurs in the leaves of plants, where chlorophyll captures energy from sunlight. The plant uses this energy to convert water and carbon dioxide into glucose and oxygen. The glucose provides energy for the plant to grow, while the oxygen is released into the air. Without photosynthesis, life on Earth would not be possible."

5. Using Cohesive Devices in Paragraphs

Example with Cohesive Devices
Without cohesive devices: "I woke up early. I went to the park. I saw my friends. We played football. We had fun. I went home."
With cohesive devices: "I woke up early and went to the park. There, I saw my friends, so we decided to play football. We had a great time together. Finally, when it started getting dark, I went home feeling happy."
Cohesive devices used: "and", "there", "so", "together", "finally", "when"

6. Practice Activity for Students

A. Identify the parts of a paragraph:
Paragraph: "My favorite subject in school is Science. It helps me understand how the world works. For example, I learned why the sky is blue and how plants grow. Science also includes fun experiments. I enjoy mixing chemicals and observing reactions. This subject makes me curious about everything around me."
Topic sentence: "My favorite subject in school is Science."
Supporting sentences: "It helps me understand how the world works. For example, I learned why the sky is blue and how plants grow. Science also includes fun experiments. I enjoy mixing chemicals and observing reactions."
Concluding sentence: "This subject makes me curious about everything around me."
B. Add cohesive devices to improve a paragraph:
Original: "I visited my grandmother. She lives in a village. The village is beautiful. It has many trees. I helped her in the garden. We cooked together. I had a good time."
Improved: "Last weekend, I visited my grandmother who lives in a village. The village is beautiful because it has many trees. While I was there, I helped her in the garden, and afterwards, we cooked together. I had a very good time during my visit."

7. Classroom Tips

Teaching Tips
  • Visual Organization: Use graphic organizers to help students plan their paragraphs with topic sentences, supporting details, and conclusions.
  • Model Writing: Demonstrate paragraph writing by thinking aloud as you compose a paragraph in front of the class.
  • Peer Review: Have students exchange paragraphs and identify the topic sentence, supporting sentences, and concluding sentence in each other's work.
  • Paragraph Scramble: Give students a paragraph with sentences in random order and have them rearrange it logically.
  • Scaffolded Writing: Provide sentence starters or frames to help students structure their paragraphs.

8. Recap of Key Points

Paragraph Structure
  • A paragraph focuses on one main idea
  • It begins with a topic sentence that introduces the main idea
  • Supporting sentences provide details, examples, or explanations
  • A concluding sentence wraps up the paragraph
  • Cohesive devices help connect sentences and ideas smoothly
Remember

A good paragraph is like a sandwich: the topic sentence and concluding sentence are the bread, and the supporting sentences are the filling in between. Make sure your paragraphs are well-structured and connected!

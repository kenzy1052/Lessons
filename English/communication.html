Formal and Informal Communication

Formal Communication

Formal communication is when we talk politely and respectfully in official or serious situations.

Examples of formal situations:
At school (talking to a teacher/headteacher)
In the workplace (interviews, business meetings)
In public offices (police station, hospital, bank)
Announcements or official speeches
Features of formal language:
No slang or street language
Complete sentences
Polite expressions (please, kindly, may I…)
Example:

"Good morning, Sir. Please, may I speak with you about my homework?"

Informal Communication

Informal communication is when we talk in a casual or relaxed way with friends, family, or classmates.

Examples of informal situations:
Talking with friends at school
Chatting with siblings at home
Playing football with teammates
Features of informal language:
Slang and short forms
Use of nicknames
Friendly tone, not too strict
Can include non-verbal expressions (smiles, gestures, handshakes)
Example:

"Hey! What's up? Are you coming to the field?"

Asking and Answering Questions in Conversations

Meaningful Questions

To keep conversations meaningful, we ask questions that make others explain more.

Question words that help get more details:
Why
How
For what reason
In what way

Example Dialogue

Conversation Example:

Ama: "Do you think girls should study engineering?"

Aziz: "Yes."

Ama: "Why do you think so?"

Aziz: "Because girls are also intelligent, and they should be given equal opportunities."

Communication Tip

This shows critical thinking and elaboration. When you ask "why" or "how," you encourage deeper thinking and more detailed responses.

Practice Activity

Turn this simple answer into a more detailed response by asking a follow-up question:

Student: "I enjoyed the school trip."

Describing Experiences

How to Describe Experiences

When telling people about your experiences:

Key elements to include:
Use correct tenses (past, present, future)
Use adjectives, adverbs, and figurative expressions to make it interesting
Include specific details about what happened, how you felt, and what you learned
Example:

"Yesterday, I went to the market. The place was very busy. The traders shouted loudly to call customers. I felt excited but also tired because of the crowd."

Situations to Describe

Common situations you might need to describe:
Visiting the hospital
Shopping at a supermarket
Travelling by bus or trotro
Attending a cultural event
Participating in a school competition

Giving and Listening to Directions

How to Give Directions

We often give directions to help people find places.

Direction words to use:
opposite
adjacent
near
turn left/right
straight ahead
a few metres away
Mention landmarks:
church
mosque
filling station
hospital
market

Example Directions

Example:

"The bank is opposite the post office. Walk straight for about 5 minutes, then turn right after the filling station."

Listening Tip

When receiving directions, listen carefully for landmarks and direction words. You can repeat the directions back to make sure you understood correctly.

Techniques for Effective Oral Communication

Voice Modulation

To communicate well, pay attention to how you use your voice:

Elements of voice modulation:
Volume - not too loud, not too low
Tone - polite and respectful
Stress - know which word to emphasize
Example:

INcrease (noun) vs. inCREASE (verb)

The word stress changes the meaning of the word.

Eye Contact

Effective eye contact:
Look at the person you are speaking to
Show interest with your face and posture
Maintain natural eye contact, not staring

Non-verbal Cues

Non-verbal communication:
Nodding, smiling, or saying "I see," "Alright," to show attention
Do not interrupt when others are speaking
Use appropriate gestures to support your message
Maintain an open posture
Communication Tip

Remember that non-verbal communication often speaks louder than words. Your body language should match what you're saying.

Quick Recall Summary

Key Points to Remember
Formal = serious situations (school, work, offices). → Polite, no slang.
Informal = relaxed situations (friends, family). → Can use slang and short forms.
Good conversations = ask "Why, How, For what reason" to get more details.
Describe experiences using correct tenses + adjectives + clear details.
Directions = use landmarks + clear words (left, right, opposite, near).
Speak well = use good voice, eye contact, and body language.
Final Tip

Good communication skills are essential for success in school and in life. Practice these skills every day in different situations.
